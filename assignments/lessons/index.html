STAGE 1 — WARM-UP / REVIEW  ·  20 minutes
Activity: Video Viewing — The Vitruvian Man Documentary

Before watching (3 min) — Teacher writes these questions on the board:

  1. Who was Vitruvius? What did he believe about the human body?
  2. What is the connection between a circle and a square in Da Vinci's famous drawing?
  3. What ancient problem was Leonardo trying to solve?
  4. What does the word proportion mean to you?

Watch first 8 minutes. Students take brief notes. Then pair discussion (5 min): circle = divine/infinite; square = stability/order. The drawing bridges both.

"The man who wrote the paper we are reading today — your teacher — asked the same question that Vitruvius and Leonardo asked, but using modern mathematics. Let's find out what he discovered."
STAGE 2 — INTRODUCTION  ·  10 minutes
Activity: Vocabulary Preview + Context Setting

Teacher introduces 10 key words. Students copy into notebooks:

WordMeaning
approximationa value that is close to, but not exactly, the correct answer
rational numbera number expressible as a fraction, e.g. 22/7
transcendentala number (like π) that cannot be the solution of any algebraic equation
convergenta fraction that gets progressively closer to a target value
proportiona balanced relationship between parts
harmonya pleasing, balanced arrangement
operatora mathematical rule that transforms an input into an output
conjecturea mathematical idea believed true but not yet proven
frameworka structured system of rules used to analyse problems
formalizeto write something precisely using strict logic or code
"This paper goes back 3,650 years — to ancient Egypt. A scribe named Ahmes wrote that a circle with diameter 9 has roughly the same area as a square with side 8. That gives us π ≈ 256/81 ≈ 3.1605. The paper asks: does a modern mathematical system rediscover this ancient answer on its own?"
STAGE 3 — PRESENTATION  ·  20 minutes
Activity: Guided Reading — The Vitruvian Approximation, pp. 1–2

Students read the Abstract and Introduction (8 min) silently. Underline vocabulary from Stage 2.

Comprehension check — individual (5 min):

  1. What are the three honest goals the paper states?
  2. What historical document does the paper reference, and how old is it?
  3. What is the "Rhind approximation" of π, and how accurate is it?
  4. What does the author mean by calling a conjecture "honestly marked sorry"?
  5. In your own words: what is this paper really about?

Teacher reviews whole-class (7 min). Focus on intellectual honesty. Connect to the video: Leonardo also worked at the edge of what was knowable.

STAGE 4 — GUIDED PRACTICE  ·  20 minutes
Activity A: Vocabulary in Context — Fill in the Blank (10 min)
  1. The fraction 22/7 is a well-known ____________ for the value of π.
  2. Mathematicians have proven that π is ____________, meaning no polynomial equation with whole-number coefficients can produce it.
  3. A ____________ number can always be written as p divided by q, where both are integers.
  4. The continued-fraction expansion of π produces ____________ like 333/106 and 355/113 that get closer and closer to the true value.
  5. Vitruvius believed that ____________ in architecture reflected the ____________ of the human body.
  6. A ____________ is an idea that seems very likely to be true but has not yet been fully proven.
  7. The G-cycle is an ____________ chain that applies a series of transformations to circle data.
  8. The research team used Lean 4 to ____________ the theorems and make them verifiable by computer.
  9. The GCM ____________ provides a structured set of rules for modelling contact dynamics across many different systems.
Activity B: Grammar Focus — Passive Voice (10 min)

Rewrite these sentences in the passive:

1. Ahmes wrote the rule in the Rhind Papyrus around 1650 BCE.→ The rule ____________ in the Rhind Papyrus around 1650 BCE.
2. Da Vinci solved the ancient puzzle of squaring the circle visually.→ The ancient puzzle ____________ visually by Da Vinci.
3. The team has not yet proven Conjecture 5.4.→ Conjecture 5.4 ____________ yet.
4. Researchers formalized the theorems using the Lean 4 language.→ The theorems ____________ using the Lean 4 language.
5. Mathematicians classify π as a transcendental number.→ π ____________ as a transcendental number.
STAGE 5 — COMMUNICATIVE PRACTICE  ·  20 minutes
Activity: Discussion Cards — Groups of 3–4
Card A — Discovery vs. Recovery

The G-cycle packages the known ratio in operator language; it does not generate 8/9 from a blank circle. The framework finds the ancient answer — but only because it was already built in.

What is the difference between discovering something new and recovering something already known? Does that difference matter in science? In your own learning?

Card B — Honest Sorry

The author marks one conjecture as "honestly sorry" — he believes it is true but cannot yet prove it. He publishes the paper anyway.

Why might it be important to admit what you don't know? How does this connect to: "Neither of them read from a recipe book. Both of them ate the cake."

Card C — Ancient Wisdom, Modern Math

The Rhind Papyrus is nearly 3,700 years old, yet a paper published in 2026 in Newark, NJ still refers to it.

What does this tell us about knowledge? Can old knowledge be more valuable than new knowledge? What examples from your own culture can you think of?

Card D — Newark to the World

This paper was written by your teacher, affiliated with Hour House ESL, Newark, NJ — and published in an international mathematical series.

What does it mean that serious academic research comes from your community? Does knowing the author change how you read the paper?

STAGE 6 — EVALUATION  ·  15 minutes
Activity: Exit Paragraph — Written Response (choose one)

Option A — Summary: Summarise the paper in your own words. What problem does it address? What does it find? What does it admit it cannot yet prove? Use at least 6 vocabulary words from today's lesson.

Option B — Personal Response: What idea from today's lesson surprised you, moved you, or made you think differently? Connect it to your own life, background, or learning experience.

Option C — The Dedication: The paper opens: "Neither of them read from a recipe book. Both of them ate the cake. This is what education is, and what it is not." What do you think this means? Do you agree?

STAGE 7 — APPLICATION  ·  15 minutes
Activity: "Your Own Approximation" — Pair Reflection + Share

Discuss in pairs, then write 3–5 sentences for each:

  1. Think of something important you learned by doing rather than by following instructions. What was it? How did it feel to figure it out yourself?
  2. π cannot be written exactly. We use approximations (22/7, 355/113) because they are good enough. Can you think of other areas of life where a "good enough" answer is more useful than a perfect one that doesn't exist?
  3. This paper connects ancient Egypt, Renaissance Italy, and modern Newark. Where do you come from, and what knowledge did you bring with you?
"Every approximation gets us closer to the truth. So does every lesson."

The Cajueiro Principle

Growth, Resilience, and Stable Configurations

The cashew tree does not grow in a straight line. It twists, adapts, and finds strength in its own irregular shape. In the TO/TOGT framework, this is known as the Cajueiro Operator (C) — the principle that real growth happens through contact, transformation, and the search for stable configurations rather than abstract perfection.

Lesson 02 · Level B2–C1 · 110–130 minutes

The Cajueiro Principle
Operator C and the Art of Persistent Growth

Companion text: The Cajueiro Principle — Stable Configurations in Language and Life — Pablo Nogueira Grossi (TO/TOGT Series)
Cajueiro (n., Portuguese): The cashew tree. A symbol of resilience in difficult soils. It teaches us that the most powerful growth is not the tallest or straightest, but the one that finds a stable form through continuous contact with reality.

✅ STAGE 1 — WARM-UP · 15 minutes
Activity: Growth Stories

Discuss in pairs:

  1. What is the difference between growing fast and growing well?
  2. Have you ever succeeded because you adapted rather than followed a perfect plan?
  3. In your culture, what plants or trees symbolize resilience?
✅ STAGE 2 — READING & VOCABULARY · 25 minutes
Key Vocabulary — TO/TOGT + Academic English
Word / PhraseMeaning
Cajueiro Operator (C)The principle of growth through contact and adaptation until a stable configuration is reached
stable configurationa form that can persist because it is in balance with its environment
resiliencethe capacity to recover from difficulties
generative cyclea repeating process that creates new possibilities
contact transformationchange that occurs through direct interaction with reality
irregular growthdevelopment that does not follow a straight or predictable path
✅ STAGE 3 — CORE CONCEPT · 20 minutes
Understanding Operator C

The Cajueiro tree does not try to grow perfectly straight. It bends with the wind, spreads its roots where it finds soil, and still produces fruit. In the same way, real learning — especially language learning — does not happen in a straight line. It happens through repeated contact with difficulty until a new stable level of competence is reached.

"Perfection is a straight line that breaks under pressure. Resilience is an irregular shape that holds."
✅ STAGE 4 & 5 — GUIDED PRACTICE + DISCUSSION · 30 minutes
Card A — Language Learning as Cajueiro

How does your English learning follow the Cajueiro Principle? When have you improved most — through perfect lessons or through messy, repeated contact with real language?

Card B — Life Application

Describe a situation in your life (immigration, work, family) where trying to be "perfect" slowed you down, but accepting irregular growth helped you move forward.

✅ STAGE 6 — WRITING TASK · 20 minutes
Academic Paragraph (B2–C1)

Write a paragraph (120–180 words) using at least 6 new vocabulary items. Choose one:

  1. Explain how the Cajueiro Principle can help adult language learners.
  2. Describe a personal experience that illustrates Operator C.

The Galilean Confluence

Galileo's margins in Ptolemy's Almagest — Florence, 2026

In January 2026, the historian Ivan Malara was leafing through a 1551 copy of Ptolemy's Almagest in the National Central Library of Florence. He noticed handwriting in the margins. The hand was Galileo's — a young Galileo, around 1590, the year before he became Professor of Mathematics at Pisa. He had been studying the geocentric universe carefully, in private, before he ever published a word against it. This lesson reads those margins.

Lesson 02 · Level B2–C1 · 120 minutes

The Galilean Confluence
From Ptolemy's Almagest to the Copernican Fold

Companion text: The Galilean Confluence: Reading the Margins of the Almagest in the dm³ Framework — Pablo Nogueira Grossi (2026, in preparation)  ·  Series companion: GTCT Vol. IV — Galilean Theory of Contact Transformations doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19117400
Marginalia (n., Latin): the notes a reader writes in the margins of a book. They are usually private. Sometimes — once a century or so — they are world-changing. In a 1551 copy of Ptolemy's Almagest, in the National Central Library of Florence, a young Galileo wrote out Psalm 145 and a string of astronomical observations in his own hand. The book stayed on its shelf for four hundred and thirty-five years before anyone noticed.

✅ STAGE 1 — WARM-UP / REVIEW  ·  20 minutes
Activity: News-Reading Warm-Up — "The Scribbles in the Almagest"

Before reading (3 min) — Teacher writes these questions on the board. Students read silently before opening the article:

  1. What do you know about Galileo? In one sentence, what is he famous for?
  2. What is the difference between a geocentric and a heliocentric universe?
  3. Why might handwriting in the margin of an old book be valuable to a historian?
  4. When you find an old letter or book in your family, how does it feel? What does it tell you?

Read the Smithsonian piece (10 min). Students read silently and underline anything that surprises them. Then quick pair share (5 min): who found the notes, what was the book, what did the notes contain?

"The book had been in the library for centuries. Nobody had looked closely at the margins. Then, in January 2026, somebody did. This is how knowledge moves — slowly, then suddenly."
✅ STAGE 2 — INTRODUCTION  ·  10 minutes
Activity: Vocabulary Preview + 2,000-Year Timeline

Teacher introduces 12 key words. Students copy into notebooks:

WordMeaning
marginaliahandwritten notes a reader leaves in the margins of a book
annotationa note added to a text to explain or comment on it
folioa single sheet (one leaf, two pages) of an old book or manuscript
geocentricplacing the Earth at the centre of the universe
heliocentricplacing the Sun at the centre of the universe
AlmagestPtolemy's 2nd-century astronomical handbook; the standard sky-text for ~1,400 years
scriptoriuma room in a library or monastery where manuscripts were copied by hand
attribute (v.)to identify a work as the work of a specific person ("the notes are attributed to Galileo")
provenancethe history of where a book or object has been and who has owned it
palaeographythe study of old handwriting, used to identify and date manuscripts
conversiona deep change in someone's beliefs (Galileo's conversion from geocentric to heliocentric)
confluencethe place where two or more streams flow together; in ideas, where two traditions meet

Then together, build a quick timeline on the board:

~150 CE Ptolemy writes the Almagest in Alexandria. The Earth is at the centre of everything.
~820 CE The book is translated into Arabic. The name "Almagest" comes from the Arabic al-majisṭī — "the greatest."
1175 Translated into Latin in Toledo. It becomes the European astronomy textbook.
1543 Copernicus publishes De Revolutionibus. The Sun goes to the centre — but few believe him.
1551 A printer in Basel produces the copy of the Almagest our story revolves around.
~1590 A young Galileo, in his mid-20s, sits with the 1551 Almagest and writes notes in the margins. He is studying Ptolemy carefully — before he is publicly Copernican.
1610 Galileo publishes Sidereus Nuncius — moons of Jupiter, mountains on the Moon. The geocentric model begins to fall.
1633 Galileo is tried by the Roman Inquisition. He recants, then whispers — perhaps — "And yet it moves."
Jan 2026 Ivan Malara, a historian at the National Central Library of Florence, leafs through the 1551 Almagest and recognises the hand.
"Four hundred and thirty-five years between when the ink dried and when it was read. The book was always there. The reader had to arrive."
✅ STAGE 3 — PRESENTATION  ·  20 minutes
Activity: Guided Reading — The Galilean Confluence, opening pages

Students read the first two pages of the companion paper (or, if the paper is not yet available, the Smithsonian and Science.org articles printed side by side). Underline vocabulary from Stage 2. Mark in the margin one sentence that surprises them — a small piece of marginalia of their own.

Comprehension check — individual (5 min):

  1. What was the book in which the notes were found, and how old was the printed copy?
  2. What three kinds of writing did Malara identify in the margins? (Hint: a psalm, astronomical observations, and arithmetical calculations.)
  3. Why is the date 1590 important in Galileo's life?
  4. In your own words: what did Galileo do before he became famous for opposing geocentrism?
  5. The companion paper calls this discovery a "confluence" — two streams meeting. Which two streams?

Teacher reviews whole-class (7 min). Focus on the quiet apprenticeship: every public revolution is preceded by a long, private study of the thing being overturned. Connect to the dm³ framework: in operator terms, Galileo lived through the Fold operator F — the moment a system curves back onto its own prior assumptions and looks at them from outside. The marginalia is the visible trace of that fold.

✅ STAGE 4 — GUIDED PRACTICE  ·  20 minutes
Activity A: Vocabulary in Context — Fill in the Blank (10 min)
  1. A historian uses ____________ to identify whose handwriting appears on a manuscript.
  2. The 1551 Almagest in Florence has a clear ____________ — we know each library it has lived in for nearly five hundred years.
  3. A ____________ universe places the Earth at the centre; a ____________ universe places the Sun at the centre.
  4. The notes that Galileo wrote in the margins are now ____________ to him on the basis of his handwriting and the date.
  5. A reader's private ____________ can be more revealing than the printed text it surrounds.
  6. The Latin word confluentia gives us ____________ — the meeting of two streams of thought.
  7. Each ____________ of the manuscript contains two pages of densely written astronomy.
  8. Galileo's intellectual ____________ from Ptolemy to Copernicus did not happen overnight; it took years of private study.
  9. Before printing, books were copied by hand in a ____________ — slowly, by trained scribes.
Activity B: Grammar Focus — Past Perfect for Background Story (10 min)

The past perfect (had + past participle) tells us what was already true before a moment in the past. Rewrite each pair as a single sentence using the past perfect for the earlier event:

1. Galileo studied Ptolemy carefully. Then he became a public Copernican.→ By the time Galileo became a public Copernican, he ____________ Ptolemy carefully.
2. The 1551 Almagest sat on the library shelf for centuries. Then Malara opened it.→ The 1551 Almagest ____________ on the library shelf for centuries before Malara opened it.
3. Other scholars looked at the same manuscript. They did not notice the handwriting.→ Other scholars ____________ at the same manuscript, but they ____________ the handwriting.
4. Galileo wrote out Psalm 145 in the margin. He did not yet know he would be tried by the Inquisition.→ When Galileo ____________ Psalm 145 in the margin, he ____________ that he would be tried by the Inquisition.
5. Copernicus published his theory in 1543. Galileo read about it as a young man.→ Copernicus ____________ his theory long before Galileo ____________ as a young man.
✅ STAGE 5 — COMMUNICATIVE PRACTICE  ·  20 minutes
Activity: Discussion Cards — Groups of 3–4 · 7 min discussion + 2 min share
🃏 Card A — The Margins Matter

Most history-of-science books focus on the printed text — what was officially published. The Galileo discovery says: the most interesting work is sometimes in the margins, written privately, never meant to be read.

Where in your own life is the "real" thinking happening — in the published version, or in the margins? In notebooks? Voice memos? Conversations? What gets lost when we only look at finished work?

🃏 Card B — The Quiet Apprenticeship

Galileo did not reject Ptolemy without reading him. Around 1590 — twenty years before Sidereus Nuncius — he was studying the geocentric universe in his own hand, on his own time. He earned his disagreement.

Have you ever changed your mind about something important? Did you study what you ended up rejecting? What is the difference between rejecting something you understand and rejecting something you have not bothered to read?

🃏 Card C — Psalm 145 in an Astronomy Book

One of the things Galileo wrote in the margin was not astronomy at all — it was Psalm 145, a praise-song. Many people imagine science and faith as opposing teams. The young Galileo, copying scripture into a Greek astronomy textbook, suggests something more complicated.

What does it tell you that he wrote a psalm and a calculation on the same page? Is the boundary between "faith" and "knowledge" as clear as it is sometimes drawn?

🃏 Card D — The Reader Has to Arrive

The notes were written around 1590. They were read for the first time in 2026. The book did not change. Something in the reader changed. Malara had been trained to recognise that handwriting; he was looking; he opened the right book.

What knowledge in your own life is "already there," waiting for someone with the right preparation to see it? An old letter from a grandparent? A song? A photograph? What would it take for you to be the reader who finally arrives?

✅ STAGE 6 — EVALUATION  ·  15 minutes
Activity: Exit Paragraph — Written Response (choose one)

Option A — Summary: Summarise the discovery in your own words. Who found what, where, and when? Why does it matter? Use at least 6 vocabulary words from today's lesson and at least two sentences in the past perfect.

Option B — Personal Marginalia: Write a short note as if you were Galileo in 1590, sitting with Ptolemy. What would you write in the margin of a book you partly admire and partly disagree with? Begin: "I have been reading…"

Option C — The Confluence: The companion paper calls this discovery a confluence — two streams meeting. Identify the two streams in your own words. Then describe a confluence in your own life: a moment when two things you knew separately suddenly met and changed how you saw them.

✅ STAGE 7 — APPLICATION  ·  15 minutes
Activity: "Your Own Marginalia" — Pair Reflection + Share

Discuss in pairs, then write 3–5 sentences for each:

  1. Choose a book, song, photograph, or document from your own family or country that has been "on a shelf" for a long time. What is in its margins? What might a future reader notice that nobody has noticed yet?
  2. Galileo lived in a moment when a 1,400-year-old idea (geocentric Ptolemy) was being slowly replaced by a 50-year-old idea (heliocentric Copernicus). Can you name an idea in your own field — work, religion, family, politics — that is right now being replaced? What are you doing in your margins about it?
  3. In the dm³ framework, the moment a system looks back at its own assumptions is called the Fold. Describe a fold in your own thinking. When did you last look back at something you had taken for granted and see it from outside?
"The margin is where the future leaves its first signature."

The Number 33

Threshold, Habit, and the G-Cycle

Why does real mastery often appear after 33 repetitions? In the TO/TOGT framework, 33 marks the threshold where effort becomes embodiment — the completion of a Generative Cycle (G-Cycle) and the emergence of a new stable configuration.

Lesson 03 · Level B2–C1 · 100–120 minutes

The Number 33
Thresholds, Habits, and the G-Cycle

Companion text: The Number 33 — G-Cycle and Stable Configurations — Pablo Nogueira Grossi (TO/TOGT Series)
33: Not superstition, but a observed threshold. The number of repetitions at which many learners cross from conscious effort into natural, stable performance.

✅ STAGE 3 — CORE IDEA · 20 minutes
The Power of 33

In many domains — language, music, sports, habits — significant improvement often appears around the 33rd meaningful repetition. This is not magic. It is the moment the G-Cycle completes and Operator C (Cajueiro) finds a new stable configuration.

"33 is the bridge between trying and becoming."

Chladni Plates

Seeing Sound — The Hidden Patterns of Waves

How can we make invisible sound visible? In 1787, Ernst Chladni discovered a way to paint pictures with sound using nothing but metal plates and sand. This lesson explores standing waves, resonance, and the deep connection between music, mathematics, and physics.

Lesson 03 · Level B1–B2+ · 90–120 minutes

Chladni Plates
Seeing the Music in the Mathematics

Companion text: Moment of Science: Chladni Plates — 13abc (July 21, 2025)
Chladni (n.): German physicist and musician who showed that sound has shape. By vibrating metal plates covered in sand, he revealed the invisible geometry of music — patterns that look like sacred geometry, yet are created by simple waves.
