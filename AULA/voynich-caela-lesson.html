About This Framework — CAELA III-C

This lesson follows the WIPPEA model from the CAELA Guide for Adult ESL Trainers (III-C: Effective Lesson Planning), based on Madeline Hunter's Direct Instruction Model (1982). The seven stages are: Warm-Up/Review → Introduction → Presentation → Guided Practice → Communicative Practice → Evaluation → Application. Each stage has a clear purpose, appropriate grouping strategy, activity type, and timing. The lesson objective is written in the CAELA format: "Students will be able to [communicative task] in order to [real-life purpose] as evidenced by [evaluation method]."

The Voynich Manuscript: History, mystery, and methods of inquiry

High Intermediate – Advanced (B2–C1 CEFR)

Reading, Listening, Speaking (with integrated Writing in Application)

120 minutes

10–25 adult learners

Individual → Pairs → Small groups → Whole class

Students will be able to discuss competing theories about an unsolved historical mystery using evidence from multiple sources, in order to participate in academic conversations and evaluate arguments critically, as evidenced by a structured group discussion and a written evaluation of one theory using hedging language appropriately.

Vocabulary: cipher, manuscript, vellum, cryptographer, decipher, herbal, conjecture, provenience Grammar: Hedging modals (may, might, could, appears to) Grammar: Passive voice for academic reporting Reading: Skimming and scanning academic summaries Speaking: Agreeing / disagreeing with evidence Writing: Topic sentence + evidence + hedge
Materials & Equipment This web page (all activities embedded) · Projector or shared screen for video · Whiteboard / markers · Handout: Hedging language reference card (see Application stage) · Student devices (optional, for quiz)
Stage 1 0:00 – 0:10 · 10 min

Warm-Up / Review

WIPPEA Stage 1
Warm-Up / Review
10 minutes

Purpose: Activate students' prior knowledge; establish the communicative context; generate interest and motivation before new material is introduced.

Pairs → Whole class

Activity: Round Robin — "The Traveler" analogy

Open with the CAELA traveler analogy to frame lesson planning and research approaches. Tell students:

"Imagine three people trying to solve a mystery. Person A has read everything, has a full plan, and knows exactly where to start. Person B has a rough idea and one lead to follow. Person C woke up this morning and decided to start solving today. Which person are you when you face a problem you've never seen before?"

Students discuss in pairs (2 min), then share (2 min whole class). Teacher briefly links all three styles to how the manuscript has been researched.

Prior Knowledge Activation — Quick Think

Ask the whole class (elicit, do not lecture): "Has anyone heard of the Voynich Manuscript? What do you know about unbreakable codes or undeciphered languages?" Write key student ideas on the board. These will be revisited at the Evaluation stage.

Comprehension Check Ask: "What's one thing you already know, and one thing you want to find out today?" — quick think-aloud, no writing required. This is a baseline before new input.
Stage 2 0:10 – 0:20 · 10 min

Introduction

WIPPEA Stage 2
Introduction — Building the Bridge
10 minutes

Purpose: Connect warm-up to new content; introduce the lesson objective explicitly; pre-teach critical vocabulary; frame the three sources students will encounter.

Whole class

1. State the Objective (2 min)

Write the objective on the board in student-friendly language: "Today we will read, watch, and discuss theories about the Voynich Manuscript so that we can evaluate arguments and practise academic discussion using hedging language." Tell students what the three sources are and what role each plays.

Present the eight key terms using context sentences drawn directly from the sources. Students match each word to its definition in pairs, then check whole-class. Do not over-explain — students will encounter the words in context.

Stage 3 0:20 – 0:45 · 25 min

Presentation — New Language and Concepts

WIPPEA Stage 3
Presentation — Input from Three Sources
25 minutes

Purpose: Deliver new content through three differentiated input channels (video → reading A → reading B), each progressively deeper. Include comprehension checks after each input segment.

Source 1 — Video Input (10 min) · Individual then Pairs

Individual

Play the TED-Ed video (4:43). Students watch with a specific listening task: "Note three facts and one theory you hear." Do not pre-explain the video content.

▶ TED-Ed: "The world's most mysterious book" — Stephen Bax (4:43)

Comprehension Check after Video (2 min) Ask students to turn to their partner and share: "What three facts did you note? Which part was a theory, not a fact?" Cold-call 2–3 pairs. Clarify any misunderstandings before moving to reading.

Source 2 — Reading A: Tiltman's NSA Report (8 min) · Individual

Individual → Pairs

Students read the summary below silently. While reading they underline: (a) one fact, (b) one inference, (c) one word that signals uncertainty. Then compare with a partner.

Reading Summary — Tiltman (NSA, declassified 2002)

Brigadier John H. Tiltman was one of Britain's greatest codebreakers. He spent over 25 years studying the Voynich Manuscript. His 1967 paper identifies three published decipherment claims — all rejected by scholars. Newbold (1921) argued Roger Bacon wrote it and had possessed a telescope centuries before its invention; his method was shown to be too subjective to yield a single verifiable text. Feely (1943) produced medieval Latin rejected as inauthentic. Strong (1946) identified the author as Anthony Askham and produced medieval English — equally unacceptable.

Tiltman's own analysis of 17 symbols found consistent patterns: paragraphs nearly always begin with specific symbols; spaces between "words" may not indicate actual word boundaries; certain groups appear to carry grammatical functions like endings or suffixes. He concluded that simple letter-for-symbol substitution is impossible because "languages simply do not behave this way." His tentative hypothesis: the text may represent a primitive synthetic universal language — a philosophical classification of ideas — similar to systems developed by Bishop Wilkins (1667) and Cave Beck (1657).

Comprehension Check (2 min) Ask: "What word did Tiltman use that shows this is a hypothesis, not a conclusion?" (Answer: "may.") Point out hedging language explicitly — this connects to the enabling skill and Evaluation stage.

Source 3 — Reading B: D'Imperio's Monograph (7 min) · Jigsaw

Jigsaw (groups of 3)

Following the CAELA Jigsaw Reading technique: divide students into groups of three. Each person reads one extract below and prepares to teach the main idea to their group in 60 seconds.

Reader 1 — Authenticity

D'Imperio surveyed all scholarly opinion. Most researchers — including Tiltman, Panofsky, and Friedman — concluded the manuscript is not a hoax. It is "a homogeneous, creative work of a serious scholar." Only Brumbaugh dissented, claiming it was a forgery to fool Emperor Rudolph II. Even he admitted there may be an underlying text.

Reader 2 — Chain of Evidence

The manuscript entered recorded history in 1665 when Marci sent it from Prague to Kircher in Rome. Before that: Rudolph II's court (before 1608), then Jacobus de Tepenecz (his erased signature, visible under UV light, confirms this). Found by Voynich at Villa Mondragone, Italy, in 1912. Now at Yale, catalogued MS 408.

Reader 3 — The Text Problem

Captain Currier found at least two different "hands" (writers) and possibly two "languages" in the manuscript. The text follows consistent internal rules throughout. Statistical patterns do not match simple substitution ciphers. No punctuation. Spaces may not mark word boundaries. These facts must be explained by any theory.

Comprehension Check (1 min) Ask one student from each jigsaw group to state the main idea of their extract in one sentence. Clarify before moving to Guided Practice.

Stage 5 1:05 – 1:25 · 20 min

Communicative Practice

WIPPEA Stage 5
Communicative Practice — Academic Discussion
20 minutes

Purpose: Allow students to integrate new language with previously acquired skills in interactive, authentic settings — moving away from teacher-controlled input toward student-generated output. Activities are less controlled than Guided Practice.

Small groups of 3–4 → Whole class report-out

Activity: Fact / Inference / Speculation — Information Gap

Each group receives the five claims below. Together they classify each as: Confirmed Fact (the source states it directly), Inference (a logical conclusion from evidence), or Speculation (no solid evidence; possible but unconfirmed). Groups must cite which source supports their classification.

  1. "The manuscript was written around 1420."
  2. "Roger Bacon wrote the Voynich Manuscript."
  3. "The manuscript was in Rudolph II's court before 1608."
  4. "The text contains meaningful content, not random nonsense."
  5. "The manuscript may represent a universal language system."

10 minutes group discussion · 5 minutes report-out · 5 minutes whole-class discussion

Language Focus — Hedging (integrate while reporting)

As groups report out, teacher models and reinforces hedging expressions. Write these on the board and ask students to use at least one when making a claim:

  • "The evidence suggests that…"
  • "It appears that…, although…"
  • "This may indicate that…"
  • "While X cannot be ruled out, the balance of evidence…"
  • "Tiltman's analysis indicates…, but this remains tentative."
Comprehension Check After report-out, ask: "Did any group disagree about a classification? What made it difficult to decide?" Use disagreement as a teaching moment about the difference between evidence and interpretation.

Evaluation

Task B — Open Written Questions (10 min) · Individual

Students answer two of the three questions in writing (4–6 sentences each). This is the primary evaluation artifact.

Evaluation Question 1Analysis

Sources: Video + Tiltman (1967)

Stephen Bax (video) and Brigadier Tiltman both tried to decode the manuscript but used completely different approaches. In your own words, explain how their methods differ and what each approach assumes about the nature of the text. Use at least one hedging expression.

Evaluation Question 2Critical

Sources: D'Imperio (1978) + Tiltman (1967)

D'Imperio reports that virtually every serious scholar concluded the manuscript is not a hoax — but Brumbaugh disagreed. Whose position do you find more convincing, and why? Use specific evidence from at least two sources and at least two hedging expressions.

Evaluation Question 3Critical

Source: D'Imperio (1978)

Captain Currier found evidence of at least two different "hands" (writers) in the manuscript. If confirmed, what are three implications this would have for existing theories about who wrote the manuscript and why? Use hedging language in each implication.

Stage 7 1:45 – 2:00 · 15 min

Application

WIPPEA Stage 7
Application — Real-World Transfer
15 minutes

Purpose: Tasks reflect real-life applications of the lesson objective. Students transfer what they have learned to a new, authentic context — moving beyond the classroom material to demonstrate genuine communicative competence.

Individual (in-class) → Take-home option

In-Class Task — 150-word Position Paragraph

Write one academic paragraph responding to this question: "After more than 600 years and dozens of failed attempts, should scholars continue trying to decode the Voynich Manuscript? Why or why not?"

The paragraph must include:

  • A clear topic sentence with your position
  • Evidence from at least two of the three sources
  • At least three different hedging expressions
  • One use of the passive voice for academic reporting (e.g. "It has been argued that…")

Wrap-Up — Return to Warm-Up (5 min)

Return to the board from the Warm-Up stage. Ask students: "What did you know at the start? What do you know now? What question do you still have?" This closes the lesson and models reflective thinking — a key CAELA skill.

Take-Home Application Activities (CAELA III-C)

Students select one of the following to complete before the next class:

  1. Find another unsolved mystery (a historical artefact, an unexplained text, an unsolved scientific question). Write a short comparison: how is the investigation similar to or different from the Voynich Manuscript research?
  2. Lesson Reflection: Write 3–5 sentences answering — What went well in your understanding today? What was most challenging? What would you still like to investigate?
  3. Research extension: Find one news article published after 2015 about the Voynich Manuscript. Summarise it in 100 words and bring it to the next class. Be ready to evaluate whether the claim in the article is a fact, inference, or speculation.

Teacher Notes — The Voynich Manuscript This lesson was used as a final project by Hour House students. Its emotional register should match a genuine intellectual challenge, not a worksheet exercise. Students who have lived through uncertainty — displacement, documentation, language barriers — often connect powerfully to a 600-year-old document that no one has been able to read.

On the interactive quiz: The quiz distinguishes between recall, analysis, and critical thinking questions. Do not skip the critical-thinking questions even with B2 students. The hardest questions often produce the best discussion. If a student chooses wrong, ask them to read the source again and identify where their reasoning diverged from the text.

On hedging language: This is the core linguistic focus of the lesson. Monitor the Application stage writing closely for inappropriate certainty: students should not write “the Voynich Manuscript is a hoax” but rather “it may be a hoax” or “the evidence appears to suggest.” This is academic writing at B2–C1 level and it matters professionally.

On the sources: Tiltman (1967) and D’Imperio (1978) are primary scholarly sources. Students should understand that these are not Wikipedia — they are the same kind of document your teacher publishes in the Principia Orthogona series. Reading them is an act of intellectual respect for the tradition they represent.

For stronger students: Ask them to write a CAELA-format lesson objective for the Voynich lesson itself, then compare it to the one in the lesson header. Does it meet the four-element standard (C, CT, P, E)?
